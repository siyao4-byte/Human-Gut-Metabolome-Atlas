# Figure 5. The human faecal atlas expands identity-resolved metabolite coverage

The human faecal atlas was compared with metabolites reported in healthy human faecal samples in HMDB and with MiMeDB metabolites. The FASST public-repository search was retained as a fourth evidence source but was not included in identity intersections because only aggregate spectrum-level search results, rather than a metabolite-identity membership list, were available.

**a,** Overview of the four collections and their evidence compatibility. Values for the human faecal atlas, healthy HMDB and MiMeDB show the number of resolved non-lipid metabolite identities used in the overlap analysis. FASST public repositories are shown separately because the available analysis describes spectrum-level matches and cannot be converted into compound-name or SMILES membership.

**b,** Venn diagram showing non-lipid metabolite-identity overlap among the human faecal atlas, healthy HMDB and MiMeDB. Standardised `compound.name` was used as the primary matching key. When a standardised name was unavailable, SMILES was used to link the record to a named identity with the same SMILES where possible; otherwise, the SMILES was retained as the identity key.

**c,** Exact intersections among the three identity-comparable non-lipid collections. Each identity contributes to one exact membership combination.

**d,** Sankey plot of identities found in the human faecal atlas but absent from both healthy HMDB and MiMeDB. Unlike the lipid-excluded Venn and UpSet summaries in **b-c**, this panel uses the full atlas-only set so that steroid-, bile-acid-like and other lipid-associated chemical space can be audited. The first layer shows the dominant NPClassifier superclasses, the second layer shows the best retained annotation-source category, and the third layer shows the retained confidence level. `Spectral neighbour` denotes analogue/neighbour-based annotation, primarily MS2Query-derived candidates; `In silico structure` denotes CSI:FingerID-derived candidates; and `In silico bile-acid library` denotes atlas-only candidates explicitly supported by the in silico bile-acid library.

Lipid exclusion was applied after identity resolution so that an identity classified as lipid in any collection was removed from all three collections. Atlas lipid assignments were based on the NPClassifier superclasses Eicosanoids, Fatty Acids and Conjugates, Fatty acyls, Fatty amides, Fatty esters, Glycerolipids, Glycerophospholipids, Octadecanoids, Sphingolipids, Steroids and terpenoid superclasses. MiMeDB lipid assignments used the corresponding database classifications, including Fatty Acyls, Glycerolipids, Glycerophospholipids, Prenol lipids, Sphingolipids and Steroids and steroid derivatives. A conservative lipid-name screen was additionally applied across all records. This exclusion improves identity comparability but may also remove some structurally related metabolites that could be matched under a more specialised lipid-nomenclature workflow.

The overlap counts are resolved comparison identities rather than the atlas-wide distinct `annotation_id` count. Multiple annotation IDs that normalize to the same standardised compound name therefore contribute once to Figure 5. For **d**, when an atlas-only identity had multiple retained evidence records, the plotted source corresponds to the best retained evidence category using the priority: in silico bile-acid library, authentic standard, direct spectral library, spectral neighbour, in silico structure and other.
